### 1. World Ocean (Marine Contamination, Ocean Impacts)

**Excerpt 1:**
> "Every year, approximately 11 million tons of plastics enter the world's oceans, which is equivalent to more than one garbage truck of plastics being dumped into the ocean every minute."

**Excerpt 2:**
> "Currently, over 200 million tons of big plastic waste (macroplastics) and approximately 35,540 tons of microplastics have accumulated in the oceans."

**Excerpt 3:**
> "The Great Pacific Garbage Patch is the largest concentration of plastics on the planet, and it exhibits steady growth. The garbage patch spans a vast area between North America and Japan. Observations indicate that in 2018, its surface area measured about 618 thousand square miles, although this figure is subject to fluctuation due to ocean currents and seasonal variability."

**Excerpt 4:**
> "Between 2015 and 2022, the average mass of plastic waste in this region grew fivefold. Of particular concern is the tenfold increase in small plastic fragments over the same period, evidencing rapid degradation of larger plastic debris."

**Excerpt 5:**
> "Microplastics have even been found at the bottom of the Mariana Trench â€” the deepest known point in the ocean. In fact, the primary site of microplastics accumulation is not the ocean surface, but the deep-sea floor. Gradually, the entire ocean floor is becoming covered with a layer of plastics."

**Excerpt 6:**
> "An estimated 94 % of ocean plastic sinks to the seabed where vast amounts of waste may remain hidden and continue to accumulate."

### 2. Atmosphere & Global Water Cycle (Airborne Plastics, Precipitation)

**Excerpt 1:**
> "Up to 25 million metric tons of micro- and nanoplastic is transported annually for thousands of kilometers by marine air, spray, snow, and fog, crossing countries, continents, and oceans."

**Excerpt 2:**
> "Each year, around 136,000 tons of microplastic is transported to coastal areas by sea breezes alone."

**Excerpt 3:**
> "When water evaporates, microplastics rise into the atmosphere from the surface of the ocean. Additionally, a combination of sea spray, wind, and waves creates air bubbles in the water that contain microplastics. When the bubbles burst, the particles are released into the atmosphere."

**Excerpt 4:**
> "Studies have shown that from autumn 2017 to summer 2019, more than 1,000 tons of plastic particles fell with precipitation across 11 national parks and reserves in the western United States. This amount is enough to produce 120 million plastic bottles."

**Excerpt 5:**
> "Plastic particles in clouds help retain more water, delaying rainfall. When rain finally occurs, it becomes more intense, as more water has accumulated in the clouds."

**Excerpt 6:**
> "A similar situation is observed in other regions of the world. For example, Quercus serrata forests in Japan, covering an area of about 32,500 kmÂ², annually capture approximately 420 trillion airborne microplastic particles in their canopies."

### 5. Entry Gates - Crisis Sources (Plastic Production, Waste Management)

**Excerpt 1:**
> "According to the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), the world now produces more than 400 million tons of plastic waste annually."

**Excerpt 2:**
> "Since the 1950s, the world has produced approximately 9,200 million metric tons of plastics. Of that, about 2,900 million tons are still in use today, including 2,700 million tons of virgin plastics and roughly 200 million tons of recycled materials. Around 5,300 million tons have ended up in landfills, while 1,000 million tons have been incinerated. An additional 1,750 to 2,500 million tons are classified as 'mismanaged.'"

**Excerpt 3:**

**Excerpt 4:**

### 6. Physical Properties (Polymer Degradation, Persistence, Characteristics)

**Excerpt 1:**
> "Depending on the type, plastics take between 100 and 1,000 years to decompose. In marine environments, especially in the cold and dark depths of the ocean, plastics break down even more slowly."

**Excerpt 2:**
> "Plastic waste is highly resistant to biodegradation, however, under the influence of waves, saltwater, and sunlight, it gradually breaks down into smaller particles â€” micro- and nanoplastics that are often invisible to the naked eye. These particles retain their polymer structure, and the degradation process continues down to the nanoscale."

**Excerpt 3:**
> "Moreover, micro- and nanoplastics contain hazardous chemicals that are added during plastic manufacturing. 'We now know that plastics contain roughly 16,000 chemicals. Of these, it is known that over 4,200 are persistent in the environment, accumulate in living organisms, are transported over long distances or pose a potential hazard,' says Annika Jahnke, author and environmental chemist at the Helmholtz Centre for Environmental Research (UFZ)."

**Excerpt 4:**
> "Plastics possess unique chemical properties that make them highly durable and resistant to degradation in the environment. Thus, thanks to their durability, versatility, and low production cost, plastics have become a major material of choice for mass manufacturing, especially for single-use items."

**Excerpt 5:**
> "As a result, having bypassed natural degradation mechanisms, plastic waste becomes a permanent component of the planet's ecosystems."
